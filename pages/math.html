JHU

With PARADIM I researched methods for on-the-fly data analysis as a means of accelerated materials discovery. As a part of this project, I developed a statistical algorithm for automatic peak detection in powder X-ray diffraction patterns. Though, in theory, this method could be used for any signal with normally distributed background noise. The benefits of an algorithm such as mine arise because they do not suffer from the "black-box" approach that many machine learning models and neural networks suffer from. Undergraduate Research Assistant, Tovar Lab

JHU

2020 - present

In the Tovar Lab at Hopkins, I studied cycloparaphenylenes (CPPs), macrocycles which are radially conjugated (in contrast to the typical linear conjugation we imagine in, say, 1,3-butadiene) and give rise to interesting optical and electronic properties. Whenver I'm asked to describe a CPP my go-to is normally, first, picture a benzene ring. Now, picture a ring that would go around your finger, that's made of benzene rings. That's a CPP!
I developed an optimized synthetic pathway towards the [8]CPP (a CPP ring with 8 benzene monomers) and used the program Gaussian16 to perform computations on the energies of the molecular orbitals of the molecule. One of the most interesting facets of CPPs is that their HOMO may be "disrupted" upon addition of aromatic substituents. This is the primary use for Gaussian, being able to compute (and visualize) the ground state energies of the molecular orbitals to determine how (if at all) the energies change when the ring is substituted.
Additionally, I synthesized a novel compound for use as a precursor to the ring-closing step of a CPP synthesis. Check out my Chemistry page for more!
